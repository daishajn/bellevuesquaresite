          <h2 class="section__title">Themes</h2>

          <p class="section__tag">AP U.S. History Connections</p>

        </div>

        <div class="cards themes-grid">

          <article class="card glass reveal">

            <h3>Work, Exchange, and Technology (WXT)</h3>

            <p>

              Postwar prosperity, the GI Bill, car ownership, and highway construction reshaped how Americans worked and

              shopped. Bellevue Square shows the shift from small, local businesses to car-centered suburban retail,

              where shopping becomes tied to technology, commuting, and rising incomes

              <span class="cite">(HistoryLink, 2015; Humes, 2006)</span>.

            </p>

          </article>

          <article class="card glass reveal">

            <h3>Geography and the Environment (GEO)</h3>

            <p>

              Bellevue’s transformation from agricultural land to a retail and office hub depended on geography:

              bridges across Lake Washington, highway routes, and available farmland shaped where and how development

              happened. The built environment replaced fields with parking lots, malls, and mid-rise buildings

              <span class="cite">(Snoqualmie Valley Historical Society, 2020)</span>.

            </p>

          </article>

          <article class="card glass reveal">

            <h3>American and Regional Culture (ARC)</h3>

            <p>

              Malls like Bellevue Square turned shopping into a cultural and social activity. The space reflects changing

              ideas of leisure, “the good life,” and modern identity, showing how consumer culture and architecture

              helped define what it meant to live in a postwar American suburb

              <span class="cite">(Cross, 2000)</span>.

            </p>

          </article>

            <h3>Developments and Processes (1A & 1B)</h3>

            <p>

              I identify and explain the major historical developments that influenced Bellevue’s transformation:

              Japanese American removal during WWII, postwar economic expansion, the GI Bill, car culture, federal

              highway spending, and the tech boom. On the website, these processes show up clearly in the timeline and

              in the “phase” cards that track Bellevue from fields → early suburb → enclosed mall → mixed-use urban

              core

              <span class="cite">(Humes, 2006; HistoryLink, 2015)</span>.

            </p>

          </article>

          <article class="card glass reveal">

            <h3>Sourcing and Situation (2A & 2C)</h3>

            <p>

              I use primary and secondary sources—archival photographs, maps, advertisements, and local histories—and

              always ask: Who created this? When? For what audience? By situating sources in their context, I can see

              how they promote certain narratives about progress, prosperity, and “modern” Eastside identity

              <span class="cite">(Densho Encyclopedia, 2019; HistoryLink, 2015)</span>.

            </p>

          </article>

    <!-- NARRATIVE -->

    <section id="narrative" class="section">

      <div class="container">

        <div class="section-header">

          <h2 class="section__title">Summary of Narrative</h2>

          <p class="section__tag">From Fields to an “Edge City”</p>

        </div>


        <div class="grid-2 narrative-grid">

          <article class="card glass narrative-main">

            <p>

              In the early 1900s, Bellevue was a rural community where Japanese American farmers cultivated strawberries

              and other crops on the land that would later become Bellevue Square. These farms anchored the local

              economy and community, shaping the early landscape of the Eastside

              <span class="cite">(Snoqualmie Valley Historical Society, 2020)</span>.

            </p>

            <p>

              World War II disrupted that continuity. In 1942, under Executive Order 9066, Japanese American families

              were forcibly removed and incarcerated. Their farms were sold, abandoned, or taken over, opening tracts of

              land for postwar redevelopment. This is a clear example of a national policy and act of discrimination

              reshaping local land ownership and community structure

              <span class="cite">(Densho Encyclopedia, 2019)</span>.

            </p>

            <p>

              In the late 1940s and 1950s, national trends—GI Bill home loans, booming car ownership, and federal

              highway spending—turned Bellevue into a desirable suburb. The Lake Washington Floating Bridge and later

              I-90/SR-520 made commuting to Seattle easier. In 1946, developer Kemper Freeman Sr. opened Bellevue

              Square as one of the region’s earliest suburban shopping centers, designed around parking, convenience,

              and modern storefronts

              <span class="cite">(HistoryLink, 2015; Humes, 2006)</span>.

            </p>

            <p>

              Through the 1960s–1980s, Bellevue’s population grew, the mall expanded, and shopping became a kind of

              social leisure tied to postwar prosperity and identity. In the 1990s and 2000s, the tech boom—especially

              companies like Microsoft—brought new wealth and global migration to the Eastside, while Bellevue Square

              evolved into The Bellevue Collection, adding hotels, restaurants, and luxury retail in a dense,

              walkable core

              <span class="cite">(Garreau, 1991; HistoryLink, 2015)</span>.

            </p>

            <p>

              Today, Bellevue Square represents both change and continuity: the buildings and brands have transformed,

              but the site still functions as a central gathering place and economic anchor. Its story mirrors national

              patterns of suburbanization, consumer culture, and urban growth, making it a powerful local lens for

              postwar U.S. history

              <span class="cite">(Cross, 2000; HistoryLink, 2015)</span>.

            </p>

          </article>


          <aside class="card glass narrative-aside">

            <h3>Phases of Change</h3>

            <ol class="phase-list">

              <li>

                <strong>Phase 1 – Fields & Farms (c. 1930s)</strong>

                <span>Japanese American strawberry farms define the local landscape and economy.</span>

              </li>

              <li>

                <strong>Phase 2 – Disruption & Removal (1942)</strong>

                <span>Internment empties farms and reshapes land ownership under EO 9066.</span>

              </li>

              <li>

                <strong>Phase 3 – Early Suburban Mall (1946–1960s)</strong>

                <span>Bellevue Square opens, oriented around cars and convenience.</span>

              </li>

              <li>

                <strong>Phase 4 – Enclosed Regional Center (1970s–1980s)</strong>

                <span>The mall expands and encloses, becoming an all-weather social space.</span>

              </li>

              <li>

                <strong>Phase 5 – Tech-Age “Edge City” (1990s–2000s)</strong>

                <span>Mixed-use density, luxury retail, and hotels reflect tech-driven growth.</span>

              </li>

              <li>

                <strong>Phase 6 – Experience Hub (2010s–2020s)</strong>

                <span>Events, dining, and experiences respond to online shopping competition.</span>

              </li>

            </ol>

            <div class="callout">

              <h4>Big Idea</h4>

              <p>

                Bellevue Square is not just “a mall”—it is evidence. It shows how policy, geography, and culture worked

                together to turn farmland into a suburban downtown.

              </p>

            </div>

          </aside>
