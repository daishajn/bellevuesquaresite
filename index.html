Research Question

Guiding Inquiry

Core Question

How did the opening and growth of Bellevue Square Mall reflect larger changes in American suburban life, consumer culture, and urban development after World War II?

Why This Question Works

  • Clear and specific but open-ended, it cannot be answered with “yes” or “no.”
  • Connects local Bellevue history to national postwar trends in suburbs, consumer culture, and urban growth.
  • Allows multiple interpretations supported by primary and secondary sources.
  • Directly targets APUSH themes and historical thinking skills about change over time and causation.

Themes

APUSH Framework

WXT — Work, Exchange, and Technology

This theme is central to the project because Bellevue Square grew out of postwar technological and economic change. New bridges, highways, and rising car ownership allowed people to live on the Eastside while working and shopping across the region. My website explores WXT through the interactive timeline and before/after slider, which show how transportation infrastructure and consumer markets turned rural farmland into a regional retail center.

GEO — Geography & the Environment

Bellevue’s physical geography and environmental reshaping are key to understanding its transformation. Early maps and photographs show open fields and berry farms, many cultivated by Japanese American families before World War II (Snoqualmie Valley Historical Society). On the site, map overlays and images compare the 1930s–1940s landscape with today’s dense urban core, illustrating how roads, bridges, and zoning changed the environment into structured commercial and residential zones.

ARC — American and Regional Culture

Bellevue Square also reflects changes in American culture: shopping shifted from a purely economic activity to a social and cultural experience. The advertisement gallery and narrative sections of the website show how the mall promoted a particular lifestyle of modern suburbia, family leisure, and seasonal events, connecting Bellevue to national patterns of consumer culture and suburban identity (Cross).

Historical Thinking Skills

Developments & Processes (Skill 1 · 1A & 1B)

The project traces major developments and processes that shaped Bellevue’s transformation: Japanese American farming, wartime removal, the postwar housing boom, highway construction, and the tech-driven urbanization of the late 20th century. The interactive timeline and “From Fields to Skyline” before/after slider visualize these developments over time and help explain how national trends like the GI Bill and car culture reshaped local land use and everyday life (Humes).

Sourcing & Situation (Skill 2 · 2A & 2C)

The site uses primary sources such as photographs of strawberry farms, wartime notices, early Bellevue Square advertisements, and later redevelopment images. In captions and explanatory text, I identify each source’s creator, intended audience, and historical situation, and I explain how those factors shape the message. This sourcing and contextualization show how different groups from Japanese American farmers to postwar developers, told very different stories about the same land (Densho Encyclopedia; HistoryLink.org).

Summary of Narrative

In the early 1900s, Bellevue’s economy relied heavily on small farms, including Japanese American owned strawberry farms that supplied fruit to markets across the region (Snoqualmie Valley Historical Society). These farms created a rural community centered on land, labor, and family networks.

During World War II, Executive Order 9066 forced Japanese American families off their land and into incarceration camps (Densho Encyclopedia). Their property was sold or taken over, clearing the way for new postwar development. This turning point shows how national wartime policy and racism directly altered local land ownership and opened space for future suburban projects.

After the war, returning veterans used GI Bill benefits to buy homes, car ownership expanded, and new bridges and highways connected the Eastside to Seattle. In 1946, Bellevue Square opened as an auto oriented suburban shopping center, with large parking lots and modern department stores that reflected postwar consumer culture and the rise of the shopping mall as a “new downtown” (HistoryLink.org).

Through the 1960s–1980s, Bellevue Square expanded and enclosed, mirroring national trends toward indoor malls. The site became a social space for families and teenagers as well as a retail hub. By the 1990s and 2000s, the tech boom and companies like Microsoft helped transform Bellevue into a dense “edge city” with office towers, luxury retail, and mixed-use developments surrounding the mall (Garreau).

Today, Bellevue Square functions as an “experience hub,” hosting events, dining, and lifestyle-focused spaces in addition to shopping. The website’s narrative shows how this single location reflects broader U.S. themes of suburbanization, economic development, cultural change, and the continuing debate over what community and public space look like in a postwar, car-centered, and increasingly digital society.

Phases of Change

  1. Fields & Farms — Japanese American strawberry farms anchor Bellevue’s rural economy.
  2. Wartime Removal (1942) — Internment removes families and clears land for new owners.
  3. Early Suburban Mall (1946) — Bellevue Square opens as a car-centered “downtown.”
  4. Enclosed Mall Era — 1970s–80s renovations match national indoor mall trends.
  5. Edge City Growth — 1990s–2000s tech boom densifies the urban core around the mall.
  6. Experience Hub — 2010s–2020s focus on events, branding, and lifestyle-based retail.

Cause → Effect

How National Forces Reshaped One Local Place

01 · Postwar Prosperity & Car Culture

After World War II, rising incomes, GI Bill benefits, and mass car ownership pushed families out of city cores and into new suburbs (Humes). Highways and bridges made it normal to drive to work, school, and shopping.

Bellevue Square’s design reflects that world: huge parking lots, wide access roads, and store entrances that face the lot instead of a traditional downtown street. The mall exists because everyday life was reorganized around the car (HistoryLink.org).

02 · Japanese American Internment & Land Ownership

Before WWII, Japanese American farmers grew strawberries and other crops across the Eastside and built tight community networks (SVHS; Densho). In 1942, Executive Order 9066 forced them into incarceration camps, and their land was sold or taken over (Daniels).

These removals cleared the way for postwar developers. The land that once supported farming families became subdivisions and, eventually, the commercial land under and around Bellevue Square.

03 · Highways, Bridges & Regional Growth

Floating bridges and interstate highways connected the Eastside to Seattle and the broader Puget Sound region (Seattle Times). Suddenly, Bellevue was no longer an edge of farmland—it was part of a daily commute network.

With better access, Bellevue became attractive for businesses and shoppers. Bellevue Square grew into a regional mall that served not just locals but visitors from across the area.

04 · Tech Boom & Rising Affluence

In the 1980s–1990s, tech companies like Microsoft drew thousands of high paying jobs to the Eastside. Office parks and corporate campuses appeared within driving distance of Bellevue Square.

As incomes rose, the mall shifted toward highe -end brands, polished interiors, and hotel and restaurant partnerships, turning “The Bellevue Collection” into a symbol of upscale suburban affluence.

05 · Zoning, Density & the “Urban Village”

City planners used zoning changes and development incentives to push more housing and offices into the downtown core. Instead of endless sprawl, Bellevue started building upward with mixed use towers.

Around Bellevue Square, this meant new condos, hotels, and office towers directly attached to the mall, turning it into the center of an urban village rather than a stand-alone shopping center.

06 · Online Shopping & the Experience Economy

As online shopping grew in the 2000s–2010s, traditional malls risked losing customers who could buy almost anything from home. To stay relevant, many centers shifted toward experiences rather than just products.

Bellevue Square responded by hosting seasonal events, pop-up installations, and interactive displays, turning in person visits into something that feels social and memorable instead of just transactional.

Eastside World-Building

What If History Went Differently?

Imagining Another Bellevue

This section uses counterfactual history to ask: What might Bellevue look like today if Japanese American farmers had not been removed during World War II and had kept control of their land?

Comparing the “actual timeline” with an imagined alternate version helps highlight how policy choices and power imbalances shaped the city’s geography, economy, and culture.

Actual Timeline: From Farms to Mall

In the real timeline, Japanese American farmers on the Eastside were removed under Executive Order 9066. Their land was sold or taken over, and after the war, developers used those parcels along with new infrastructure and zoning to build subdivisions and eventually Bellevue Square and its surrounding retail core (Densho; HistoryLink.org).

Bellevue became a classic postwar suburb and then an “edge city,” with the mall and nearby office towers symbolizing prosperity, consumption, and high tech growth.

Alternate Timeline: Farming City with a Different Center

In an alternate version of history, Japanese American families return from the war and reclaim or keep their land. Instead of being converted quickly into suburban tracts, large portions of the Eastside remain in small scale agriculture and family run food businesses.

Bellevue might still develop a commercial center, but it could revolve around a public market, farm co-ops, and agricultural festivals rather than a car-oriented enclosed mall. The skyline might be lower, and local food economies and cultural traditions would stay more visible in the built environment.

Why This “What If?” Matters

Counterfactuals are not about pretending we can rewrite the past; they help reveal how much of the present is the result of specific choices and power structures. Imagining a Bellevue where Japanese American farmers kept their land makes it easier to see what was lost—not just property, but community networks, traditions, and alternative futures for the city’s development.

This reflection connects back to the project’s main question: Bellevue Square’s story is not just about one mall. It is about whose stories became central, whose were pushed aside, and how geography, race, and policy interacted to shape the Eastside we see today.
